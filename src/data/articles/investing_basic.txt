TITLE: What is a Stock?
A stock represents ownership in a company. When you buy a share of Apple stock, you own a tiny piece of Apple Inc. Companies sell stocks to raise money to grow their business. As a shareholder, you benefit when the company grows and its stock price rises. You may also receive dividends, which are portions of company profits paid to shareholders. Stocks are traded on exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and NASDAQ. Stock prices change constantly based on supply and demand, company performance, and overall market conditions. Investing in stocks carries risk — prices can go down as well as up.

TITLE: What is Diversification?
Diversification means spreading your investments across different assets to reduce risk. The idea is simple: don't put all your eggs in one basket. If you own stock in only one company and it goes bankrupt, you lose everything. But if you own stocks in 20 different companies across different industries, one company failing won't ruin your portfolio. Diversification can be achieved through stocks across different sectors (technology, healthcare, energy), different asset types (stocks, bonds, real estate), and different geographies (US, international, emerging markets). ETFs and mutual funds make diversification easy by pooling many investments together.

TITLE: What is an ETF?
An ETF, or Exchange-Traded Fund, is a collection of investments bundled together and traded on a stock exchange like a single stock. For example, SPY is an ETF that tracks the S&P 500 index — when you buy one share of SPY, you effectively own tiny pieces of 500 large US companies. ETFs offer instant diversification at low cost. Most ETFs have very low expense ratios (annual fees), often below 0.1%. ETFs can track stock indexes, bond markets, commodities like gold, or specific sectors like technology. They are one of the most recommended investment vehicles for beginners because of their simplicity, low cost, and built-in diversification.

TITLE: What is Compound Interest?
Compound interest is earning interest on your interest — it is one of the most powerful concepts in personal finance. For example, if you invest $1,000 at 10% annual return: After year 1 you have $1,100. After year 2 you have $1,210 (not $1,200) because you earned 10% on $1,100. After 30 years that $1,000 grows to over $17,000 without adding another penny. The key factors are: the interest rate (higher is better), time (longer is much better), and frequency of compounding (more frequent compounding means faster growth). This is why starting to invest early — even small amounts — is so powerful. Time in the market matters more than timing the market.

TITLE: What is Risk Tolerance?
Risk tolerance is your ability and willingness to handle losses in your investments. It depends on your financial situation, investment timeline, and personality. Investors are generally categorized as conservative (prefer safe investments, cannot stomach losses), moderate (balance between safety and growth), or aggressive (comfortable with large swings in value for potentially higher returns). Your risk tolerance should match your timeline — if you need money in 2 years, you should be conservative. If you won't need the money for 30 years, you can afford to be more aggressive because you have time to recover from market downturns. Age is a common factor — younger investors typically can take more risk than older investors near retirement.

TITLE: What are Bonds?
A bond is a loan you make to a company or government. When you buy a bond, you are lending money and in return receive regular interest payments (called the coupon) and your principal back at the end of the bond's term (called maturity). Bonds are generally safer than stocks but offer lower returns. US Treasury bonds are considered among the safest investments in the world because they are backed by the US government. Corporate bonds pay higher interest but carry more risk. Bond prices move inversely to interest rates — when rates rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa. Bonds play an important role in a diversified portfolio, providing stability and income to balance the volatility of stocks.
