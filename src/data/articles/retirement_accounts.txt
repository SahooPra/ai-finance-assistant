TITLE: What is a 401(k)?
A 401(k) is a retirement savings account offered by employers that provides significant tax advantages. You contribute pre-tax dollars directly from your paycheck, reducing your taxable income today. For example, if you earn $60,000 and contribute $6,000 to your 401k, you only pay income tax on $54,000. Your investments grow tax-deferred, meaning you pay no taxes until you withdraw the money in retirement. The 2024 contribution limit is $23,000 per year (plus $7,500 catch-up if you are 50 or older). Many employers offer matching contributions — free money added to your account, often 50 cents or $1 for every dollar you contribute up to a certain percentage. Always contribute at least enough to get the full employer match — it is an instant 50-100% return on that money.

TITLE: What is a Roth IRA?
A Roth IRA is an individual retirement account where you contribute after-tax dollars. The key benefit is that your money grows completely tax-free and qualified withdrawals in retirement are also tax-free. For example, if you invest $6,000 today and it grows to $60,000 over 30 years, you pay zero taxes on that $54,000 of growth when you withdraw it in retirement. The 2024 contribution limit is $7,000 per year ($8,000 if you are 50 or older). There are income limits — in 2024, single filers earning over $161,000 and married filers earning over $240,000 cannot contribute directly. A Roth IRA is generally better for younger people who expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement. You can withdraw your contributions (not earnings) at any time without penalty, making it somewhat flexible.

TITLE: Traditional IRA vs Roth IRA
The main difference between a Traditional IRA and Roth IRA is WHEN you pay taxes. Traditional IRA: you contribute pre-tax money (tax deduction now), the money grows tax-deferred, and you pay income taxes when you withdraw in retirement. Roth IRA: you contribute after-tax money (no deduction now), the money grows tax-free, and withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. Choose Traditional if you are in a high tax bracket now and expect a lower bracket in retirement. Choose Roth if you are in a low tax bracket now and expect a higher bracket in retirement — which is typical for younger workers early in their career. Both have the same $7,000 annual contribution limit in 2024. Many financial educators recommend Roth for most young investors because of the tax-free growth benefit over decades.

TITLE: What is Dollar-Cost Averaging?
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals regardless of market conditions. For example, investing $200 every month into an S&P 500 ETF no matter what the market is doing. When prices are low, your $200 buys more shares. When prices are high, your $200 buys fewer shares. Over time this averages out your purchase price and reduces the risk of investing a large sum at the wrong time. DCA removes emotion from investing — you do not have to worry about timing the market. It is the strategy used automatically by most 401(k) plans through regular payroll contributions. Studies show that for most individual investors, consistent DCA beats trying to time the market.

TITLE: What is an Emergency Fund?
An emergency fund is money set aside for unexpected expenses or financial hardship such as job loss, medical bills, or car repairs. Financial experts recommend having 3 to 6 months of living expenses saved in a liquid, easily accessible account like a high-yield savings account. For example, if your monthly expenses are $3,000, your emergency fund should be $9,000 to $18,000. An emergency fund prevents you from going into debt or selling investments at a bad time when unexpected costs arise. Building an emergency fund should be your first financial priority before investing. Keep it separate from your checking account to avoid spending it, but accessible — not locked in a CD or invested in stocks where the value can drop right when you need it most.
